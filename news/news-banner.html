There are some new faces around Eureka College these days reflecting the institution's storied past. The faces appear on maroon banners depicting 20 prominent alumni from the college's 160-year history. The banners hang from lampposts dotting the campus.

College officials selected certain alumni from throughout the decades, but, "The men and women pictured on the banners really represent all of the Eureka College alumni who have and who are making a difference in the world," Vice President of Institutional Advancement Mike Murtagh said.

Alumni range from the Class of 1860's Elijah Dickinson, who was the first graduate, to 1999 grad Brad Sutton, a professor and cardiologist.

Other alumni include 1924 graduate Durward Sandifer, who is credited with providing most of the wording for the U.N. Declaration of Human Rights; 1932 graduate Ronald Reagan, who served two terms as U.S. President; 1932 graduate Willie Sue Smith Stewart, who was the first African-American woman to graduate from the college and had a 50-year career as an educator; and 1947 graduate Irmgard Rosenzweig Wessel, who escaped Nazi Germany and had a 60-year career in social work.

Others include 1948 graduate Emik Avakian, who was afflicted with cerebral palsy and who patented devices for the physically handicapped, including a breath-operated computer, and 1992 graduate Janelle Miller Reents, who worked at the Washington, Ill., Monical's Pizza and now is president of that company.

Other professions represented are U.S. Congressman, state governor, philanthropist, suffragist, missionary, educator, coach, scientist, U.S. Army cryptographer, jazz musician and opera singer.

"Eureka College has graduated men and women of character and integrity," Murtagh said. "They have been good mothers and fathers, successful business people, doctors, lawyers, people of faith and good citizens, serving their communities, our nation and, sometimes, the entire world."
